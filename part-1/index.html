Stop Doing Pedophile Math

Stop letting clinical terms become an escape route for abusing children.

Dr. Yamicia Connor, MD, PhD, MPH · June 2, 2026
Use this when Use this when someone tries to minimize what was done to Epstein’s victims by pointing out their ages, their appearance, their apparent maturity, or the clinical distinction between pedophilia and ephebophilia.
“They didn’t care that I was 14. They didn’t even ask.”

— Epstein survivor testimony, 2024

Megyn Kelly has a lot of opinions about a lot of things. In November 2025, one of those opinions was about Jeffrey Epstein. Specifically, about the clinical terminology we use to describe men who sexually abuse children.

The discussion went roughly like this: Epstein was not technically a pedophile, because pedophilia refers to attraction to prepubescent children, and many of Epstein’s known victims were adolescents—fourteen, fifteen, sixteen years old. The clinically precise term, Kelly suggested, might be ephebophilia. Or hebephilia. The argument was presented as a contribution to accuracy.

I want to be clear about what this is: it is pedophile math.

And I am not doing it.

The Taxonomy Argument

Let me give the argument its due. The clinical terms are real. DSM-5 defines pedophilic disorder as recurrent, intense sexual urges or fantasies involving prepubescent children—generally age thirteen and under. Hebephilia refers to attraction to early pubescent children, roughly ages eleven to fourteen. Ephebophilia refers to attraction to mid-to-late adolescents, roughly ages fifteen to nineteen.

These distinctions exist because they have clinical utility—in research, in forensic assessment, in treatment planning. They help clinicians and researchers understand patterns of offending behavior so we can intervene and prevent harm.

They are not designed to be deployed in public commentary to suggest that abusing a fifteen-year-old is categorically different from abusing a nine-year-old in ways that should matter to us morally.

But that is exactly how Megyn Kelly used them. And she is not alone.

Every time the Epstein case is discussed in public, someone performs this math. Someone notes that sixteen is different from six. Someone observes that the victims were “teenagers” rather than “little kids.” The implication—usually unspoken, sometimes quite explicit—is that this changes something. That it’s less serious. That it’s more ambiguous. That maybe the girls bear more responsibility than we’d like to say.

This is not clinical precision. This is the construction of an escape route.

What Kelly Actually Said

Let me be specific, because specificity matters when someone is parsing the ages of sexually abused children on national radio.

On November 12, 2025, on her SiriusXM show, Kelly cited an unnamed source “very, very close to this case” and said: “This person has told me from the start, years and years ago, that Jeffrey Epstein, in this person’s view, was not a pedophile.”

She continued: “He was into the barely legal type. Like, he liked fifteen-year-old girls. And I realize this is disgusting. I’m definitely not trying to make an excuse for this. I’m just giving you facts—that he wasn’t into, like, eight-year-olds.”

And then: “You can say that’s a distinction without a difference. I think there is a difference. There’s a difference between a fifteen-year-old and a five-year-old, you know?”

She also referred to victims as “young women” and described them as people who “could pass for even younger than they were, but would look legal to a passer-by.”

Kelly never used the clinical terms hebephilia or ephebophilia. She didn’t need to. She was doing the same work those terms do when they’re pulled out of forensic settings and dropped into public conversation: creating gradients of harm. Suggesting that one end of the gradient is “abnormal” and the other end is “basically normal.”

Fifteen is below the age of consent in every U.S. jurisdiction. In Florida, where much of Epstein’s abuse occurred, the age of consent is eighteen. “Barely legal” is a phrase that describes a category of pornography, not a fifteen-year-old girl.

The Response

The backlash was bipartisan, visceral, and sustained in ways that surprised even people who follow these stories closely.

Epstein survivor Marina Lacerda said on Alex Wagner’s podcast: “Don’t say that a fourteen-year-old is not like an eight-year-old. Please, please don’t do that. Don’t normalize the fact that we weren’t eight years old.”

Eight survivors partnered with World Without Exploitation to release a national PSA showing photographs of themselves at ages fourteen through seventeen, ending with: “It’s time to bring the secrets out of the shadows.” An unnamed survivor wrote a first-person essay in HuffPost, saying Kelly’s comments convinced her to come forward publicly after thirty years of silence.

Actress Valerie Bertinelli posted a photo of herself at fifteen with the caption “I’M FIFTEEN. I’M A CHILD,” sparking the viral #iWasFifteen movement. Christina Ricci called Kelly “a danger to children.” Conservative commentator Mark Levin—not exactly a liberal ally—called her a “lowlife” and a “crackpot.” John Oliver devoted a segment to what he called Kelly’s “pedophile math.”

A fourteen-year-old named Eloise posted a viral TikTok: “The minute adults start defending predators by debating the age of a child, you’re not protecting the truth. You’re protecting the predator.”

Kelly did not apologize. She made no formal response. SiriusXM refused to condemn her remarks. She kept her show. She kept covering the Epstein story.

A fourteen-year-old girl understood what Kelly would not say. That tells you everything about who the taxonomy argument actually serves.

How the Medical Establishment Built This Permission Structure

Here is the part that is harder for me to say, because I am a physician, and this implicates my profession.

Kelly was not inventing her argument from nothing. She was using the permission structure that the medical establishment built.

When hebephilia was proposed for inclusion in the DSM-5 by Ray Blanchard’s paraphilias subworkgroup as part of a combined “pedohebephilic disorder” diagnosis, the opposition was fierce. Allen Frances, who chaired the DSM-IV task force, and Michael First, its editor, published a landmark 2011 paper arguing that sexual attraction to pubescent youth is “not the slightest bit abnormal or unusual”—biologically normative, even if acting on it is criminal. Forensic psychologist Karen Franklin called the proposed diagnosis “diagnostic pretextuality,” warning it was being manufactured to justify indefinite civil detention.

The DSM-5 committee rejected it. Symbolic votes at two major professional conferences produced overwhelming majorities against inclusion. Psychiatrists knew exactly how this framework would be weaponized. They knew it would be used to civilly commit offenders indefinitely and to minimize harm by saying “well, it’s not technically pedophilia.”

They saw both dangers. They rejected the diagnosis.

But the terms did not go away. And the framework did not disappear.

Fred Berlin of Johns Hopkins has noted that “most men can find persons in this age group sexually attractive, but that doesn’t mean they’re going to act on it.” The clinical community was trying to be precise. But once you create categories, you create gradients. Once you create gradients, you create hierarchies. And once you create hierarchies, someone will use them to argue that one end is “normal” and the other end is “real abuse.”

That is exactly what happened.

The forensic misuse cuts both ways. Frances and First documented government evaluators using “paraphilia NOS, hebephilia” to justify lifelong involuntary psychiatric commitment of sex offenders. Franklin documented defense attorneys citing ephebophilia to argue diminished responsibility. Both uses distort clinical science for instrumental purposes.

And then Megyn Kelly went on national radio and used the same framework to suggest that abusing a fifteen-year-old is categorically different from abusing a five-year-old in ways that should change how we think about Jeffrey Epstein.

The taxonomy argument is not really about clinical accuracy. It is about finding a way to talk about the abuse of children without having to fully reckon with it.

What I Know From Clinical Practice

I work with pregnant teenagers. Fifteen-year-olds navigating pregnancy, childbirth, parenthood. Adult consequences in children’s bodies.

They are in situations that require adult decision-making, but their brains—particularly the prefrontal cortex that governs judgment, risk assessment, long-term thinking—will not be fully developed until their mid-twenties.

At fourteen or fifteen, adolescents are seeking independence while still needing guidance and approval. They want to be seen as mature, as special, as chosen. They are learning to navigate their own sexuality while lacking the cognitive tools to fully assess risk or power differentials.

They are, in every meaningful sense, children.

When a fifteen-year-old girl is exploited by a fifty-year-old billionaire, I do not reach for a diagnostic subcategory to describe the man’s attraction pattern. I see a child who was harmed. That is what the clinical reality shows. That is what the developmental science confirms. That is what the law recognizes.

The clinical terms give us a way to avoid that simplicity. They give us a vocabulary for making it technical, academic, above-the-fray. They let us avoid the simpler and more uncomfortable truth:

These were children. People who knew they were children chose to exploit them. And powerful institutions—legal, medical, financial, political—made the choice to let it continue.

I do not need a new term for that. I need us to stop pretending the old ones are the problem.

The taxonomy argument is not really about clinical accuracy. It is about finding a way to talk about the abuse of children without having to fully reckon with it.
