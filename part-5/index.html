Part Five · The Epstein Files Series

You Don’t Need a Cabal. You Just Need a World That Doesn’t Care.

The Epstein case is not an aberration. It is the ordinary functioning of power when it does not fear consequences.

Dr. Yamicia Connor, MD, PhD, MPH · In Her Name · Labora Signal · June 16, 2026
Use this when Use this when you need the full argument — when someone wants to understand not just what happened but what it means, and what accountability actually requires.

The Epstein case will eventually leave the news cycle. The files will be archived. The public attention will shift. Some people will continue following the accountability thread, and most will not.

Before that happens, I want to say something about what the Epstein case reveals—not about Jeffrey Epstein, but about us. About the society that produced him, protected him, and continues to elect and confirm the people who facilitated his impunity.

You do not need a secret cabal to explain the Epstein case. You do not need tunnels or coded messages or ritual evil. You need something much simpler and much harder to fix.

You need a world in which adolescent girls do not matter enough.

The Through-Line

I have been practicing medicine for years. I have worked in systems that were supposed to protect women and girls. I have watched those systems fail, consistently, in ways that are almost never dramatic.

The failure is usually quiet. It looks like a nurse who does not quite believe a teenage patient when she describes pain. It looks like a doctor who documents “anxiety” when a woman presents with symptoms that, in a male patient, would prompt immediate investigation. It looks like a system that assumes women are exaggerating, overreacting, seeking attention, failing to understand their own bodies.

The Epstein case is not a departure from this pattern. It is the extreme end of a continuum. At one end: the low-level, normalized dismissal of girls’ and women’s experiences that I encounter in clinical practice every day. At the other end: a billionaire running a pyramid scheme of child sexual abuse with the cooperation of the world’s most powerful institutions.

The distance between those two ends is not as great as we would like it to be.

What the Case Reveals About Power

The Epstein case is a map. Not of conspiracy, but of how power actually operates when it does not fear consequences.

Power did not hide. Epstein hosted scientists, politicians, and billionaires at his properties. The gatherings were photographed. The guest lists were maintained. The flight logs were recorded. This was not a secret operation—it was a social one.

Power was not ashamed. After his 2008 conviction, Epstein continued to host events, maintain relationships, and receive visitors. The men who visited him knew he was a convicted sex offender. Their continued association was not an oversight. It was a statement about what they believed they could get away with.

Power protected itself. The non-prosecution agreement shielded all co-conspirators. The plea deal reframed child victims as prostitutes. The files were released in a way that exposed victims while protecting powerful men. At every step, the system functioned exactly as designed—to absorb the shock of exposure without producing accountability.

And power continues to operate. The men named in the files hold positions of authority. They control trade policy, monetary policy, media platforms, charitable foundations, sports franchises, royal estates. Their association with a convicted child sex trafficker has not materially affected their power, their wealth, or their influence.

That is not a failure of the system. That is the system working as intended.

What I Want to Leave You With

When I started writing this series, I wanted to be careful. I wanted to stick to the documented record. I wanted to distinguish between what is proven and what is alleged, between what the files show and what conspiracy theorists have projected onto them. I wanted to resist the pull of righteous anger in favor of precision.

I have tried to do that. But I also want to say something that precision alone cannot capture.

The Epstein case is not fundamentally about Jeffrey Epstein. It is not about any single predator or any single network. It is about the fact that our society—the one we built, the one we participate in, the one we vote in and raise children in—does not protect girls.

Not just from trafficking. From dismissal. From exploitation. From the quiet, persistent message that their pain matters less, their testimony carries less weight, their bodies are less theirs.

The fourteen-year-old who said “they didn’t care that I was fourteen” was not just describing Jeffrey Epstein’s household. She was describing the plea deal. The continued relationships. The cabinet appointments. The election results. The files release that exposed victims while protecting perpetrators.

She was describing the world we made.

I do not have a clean ending for this series. I do not have a call to action that feels proportionate to what the files reveal. I know that “hold powerful men accountable” is true but insufficient, because the mechanisms of accountability are controlled by the very men who need to be held accountable.

What I have is this: as a physician, I will continue to see fifteen-year-old patients and treat them as what they are—children. I will continue to practice trauma-informed care. I will continue to recognize the signs of exploitation and trafficking. I will continue to believe girls when they describe their own experiences, even when—especially when—the full picture seems too terrible to be real.

I will not do pedophile math. I will not parse the ages of sexually abused children into diagnostic subcategories that make us more comfortable. I will not participate in the pretense that a fifteen-year-old who was recruited from her high school and paid to have sex with a billionaire was exercising choice.

I will name it. I will keep naming it.

And I will ask the question that the girl who said “they didn’t care” was really asking:

Do we?

The Epstein files are not an aberration. They are a map. They show us how power operates when it does not fear accountability. They show us what happens when society sees fourteen-year-old girls not as children who deserve protection but as objects who can be used.

And they show us that we have always known.
